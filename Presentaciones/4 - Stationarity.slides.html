Stationarity¶

Stationarity is an important concept in the field of time series analysis with tremendous influence on how the data is perceived and predicted. When forecasting or predicting the future, most time series models assume that each point is independent of one another.

Strictly Stationary Time Series¶

A strictly stationary time series is one for which the probabilistic behavior of every collection of random variables

$$ \{ X_{t_1}, X_{t_2}, \cdots, X_{t_k} \} $$

is identical to that of the time shifted set

$$ \{ X_{t_1+h}, X_{t_2+h}, \cdots, X_{t_k+h} \} $$

That is,

$$ Pr(X_{t_1} \leq c_1, X_{t_2} \leq c_2, \cdots, X_{t_k} \leq c_k \}) = Pr(X_{t_1+h} \leq c_1, X_{t_2+h} \leq c_2, \cdots, X_{t_k+h} \leq c_k) $$

for all $k=1,2,\cdots$, all time points $t_1, t_2, \cdots, t_k$, all numbers $c_1, c_2, \cdots, c_k$, and all time shifts $h=0, \pm 1, \pm 2, \cdots$

Weakly Stationary Time Series¶

A weakly stationary time series, $X_t$, is a process such that:

  1. the mean value function, $\mu_t$, is constant and does not depend on time $t$ ,
  2. the variance $\sigma_{X_t}$ is ﬁnite for all $t$, and
  3. the autocovariance function, $\gamma(s, t)$, depends on $s$ and $t$ only through their difference $|s-t|$.

Henceforth, we will use the term stationary to mean weakly stationary; if a process is stationary in the strict sense, we will use the term strictly stationary.

Autocovariance and Autocorrelation of a Stationary Time Series¶

The autocovariance function of a stationary time series will be written as

$$ \gamma(h) = \text{cov}(X_{t+h}, X_t) = \mathbb{E}[(X_{t+h}\mu)(X_t-\mu)] $$

The autocorrelation function of a stationary time series will be written as

$$ \rho(h) = \frac{\gamma(y+h,t)}{\sqrt{\gamma(t+h,t+h) \gamma(t,t)}}=\frac{\gamma(h)}{\gamma(0)} $$

Trend Stationarity¶

A stochastic process is trend stationary if an underlying trend (function solely of time) can be removed, leaving a stationary process. Meaning, the process can be expressed as

$$ Y_t=f(t) + X_t $$

where $f(t)$ is any function $f: \mathbb{R} \rightarrow \mathbb{R}$ and $X_t$ is a stationary stochastic process with a mean of zero.

Seasonal Stationarity¶

A stochastic process is seasonal stationary if the joint distribution of any set of samples is invariant over a time shift of $mP$, where $m \in \mathbb{Z}$ and $P \in \mathbb{N}$ is the period of the process:

$$ Pr(X_{t_1}, \cdots, X_{t_n}) = Pr(X_{t_{1+mP}}, \cdots, X_{t_{n+mP}}) $$

Importance of Stationarity for Estimations¶

Although the theoretical autocorrelation and cross-correlation functions are useful for describing the properties of certain hypothesized models, most of the analyses must be performed using sampled data.

From the point of view of classical statistics, this poses a problem because we will typically not have i.i.d. copies of $X_t$ that are available for estimating the covariance and correlation functions.

In the usual situation with only one realization, however, the assumption of stationarity becomes critical.

Expected Value Estimation¶

Sometimes we don't count with the probability distributions functions, and the only available information is a set of samples from that distribution (or a set of samples with the same mean), then to estimate te mean we will use

$$ \bar{X} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} X_i $$

Here, $\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}]=\mu$, and the standard deviation (error) of the estimate is the square root of $\text{var}(\bar{X})$.

Variance Estimation¶

In many cases we need to estimate the variance from a set of samples, here we have two variants. The first of them is named biased variance, and it is computed as:

$$ s^2(X)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(X_i-\bar{X})^2 $$

and the second one is named unbiased variance, computed as:

$$ s^2(X)=\frac{1}{N-1}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(X_i-\bar{X})^2 $$

Biased Variance¶

The bias of the first definition is caused because

$$ \mathbb{E}[s^2]=\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}(X_i-\bar{X})^2\right] =\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbb{E}\left[ \left(X_i-\bar{X}\right)^2 \right] \\ =\frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \left(\mathbb{E}[ X_i^2 ]-\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}^2]\right) =\frac{1}{N} \left( \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbb{E}[ X_i^2 ]- N\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}^2] \right) \\ =\frac{1}{N} \left( N \mathbb{E}[ X_1^2 ]- N\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}^2] \right) =\mathbb{E}[ X_1^2 ] - \mathbb{E}[\bar{X}^2] \\ =(\mathbb{E}[ X_1^2 ] - \mathbb{E}[ X_1 ]^2 ) + \mathbb{E}[ X_1 ]^2 - (\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}^2] - \mathbb{E}[\bar{X}]^2) - \mathbb{E}[\bar{X}]^2 \\ = \text{var}(X_1) + \mu^2 - \text{var}(\bar{X}) - \mu^2 = \text{var}(X_1) - \frac{1}{N}\text{var}(X_1) \\ = \frac{N-1}{N}\text{var}(X_1) = \frac{N-1}{N}\sigma^2 $$

Sample Autocovariance Function¶

The sample autocovariance function is deﬁned as:

$$ \hat{\gamma}(h)=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{t=1}^{N-h}(X_{t+h}-\bar{X})(X_t-\bar{X}) $$

with $\hat{\gamma}(h)=\hat{\gamma}(-h)$ for $h=0,1,\cdots,N-1$.

As in variance, this is a bias estimation, the unbiased one is divided by $N-h-1$. Another estimation could be divided by $N-h$. The advantage of the estimation described above is that it is a non-negative definite function.

The autocovariance function, $\gamma(h)$, of a stationary process is non-negative definite ensuring that variances of linear combinations of the variates $X_t$ will be never negative, then, it estimate shouldn't be negative either.

Sampled Autocorrelation Function¶

The sample autocorrelation function is deﬁned as:

$$ \hat{\rho}(h)=\frac{\hat{\gamma}(h)}{\hat{\gamma}(0)} $$

Large-Sample Distribution of the ACF¶

Under general conditions*, if $X_t$ is white noise, then for $n$ large, the sample ACF, $\hat{\rho}_X(h)$, for $h=1,2,\cdots,H$, where H is ﬁxed but arbitrary, is approximately normally distributed with zero mean and standard deviation given by:

$$ \sigma_{\hat{\rho}_X} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} $$

Based on the previous result, we obtain a rough method of assessing if peaks in $\hat{\rho}{h}$ are signiﬁcant by determining if they are out of the interval $\pm 2\sigma_{\hat{\rho}_X}$.

The applications of this property develop because many statistical modeling procedures depend on reducing a time series to a white noise series using various kinds of transformations.

Cross-functions Estimations¶

The estimator for the cross-covariance function, $\gamma_{XY}(h)$ is given by

$$ \hat{\gamma}_{XY}(h) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{t=1}^{N-h}(X_{t+h}-\bar{X})(Y_t-\bar{Y}) $$

and the estimator for the cross-correlation, $\rho_{XY}(h)$ is given by

$$ \hat{\rho}_{XY}(h)=\frac{\hat{\gamma}_{XY}(h)}{\sqrt{\hat{\gamma}_X(0) \hat{\gamma}_Y(0)}} $$

Large-Sample Distribution of Cross-Correlation¶

The large sample distribution of $\hat{\rho}_{XY}(h)$ is normal with mean zero and

$$ \sigma_{\hat{\rho}_{XY}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}} $$

if at least one of the processes is independent white noise.
