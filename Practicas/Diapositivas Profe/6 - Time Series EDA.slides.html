Time Series Exploratory Data Analysis¶

With time series data, it is the dependence between the values of the series that is important to measure; we must, at least, be able to estimate autocorrelations with precision.

It would be dicult to measure that dependence if the dependence structure is not regular or is changing at every time point. Hence, to achieve any meaningful statistical analysis of time series data, it will be crucial that the mean and the autocovariance functions satisfy the conditions of stationarity.

Interpolation¶

In many cases, we will have unknown values during our sampling process. Usually, we approximate those missing values with a reasonable value obtained from the data points around it; in the time series analysis, that could be a valid value. That is the interpolation process.

Understanding the problem is essential to decide the best interpolation method.

Linear Interpolation¶

In mathematics, linear interpolation is a method of curve fitting using linear polynomials to construct new data points within the range of a discrete set of known data points.

If the two known points are given by the coordinates $(t_0, X_{t_0})$ and $(t_1, X_{t_1})$, the linear interpolant is the straight line between these points. For a value $X_t$ in the interval $(t_0, t_1)$, the value $X_t$ along the straight line is given from the equation of slopes

$$ \frac{X_t - X_{t_0}}{t-t_0}=\frac{X_{t_1} - X_{t_0}}{t_1-t_0} $$

Solving this equation for $X_t$, which is the unknown value at $t$, gives

$$ X_t=\frac{X_{t_0}(t_1 - t) + X_{t_1}(t - t_0)}{t_1 - t_0} $$

Variance Stabilization¶

Often, obvious aberrations are present that can contribute nonstationary as well as nonlinear behavior in observed time series. In such cases, transformations may be useful to equalize the variability over the length of a single series. A particularly useful transformation is

$$ Y_t = log X_t , $$

which tends to suppress larger fluctuations that occur over portions of the series where the underlying values are larger.

Power Transformations¶

Other possibilities for variance stabilization are power transformations in the Box-Cox family of the form

$$ Y_t = \begin{cases} \frac{X_t^\lambda - 1}{\lambda} & \lambda \neq 0 \\ log(X_t) & \lambda=0 \end{cases} $$

Often, transformations are also used to improve the approximation to normality or to improve linearity in predicting the values of one series from antoher.

Smoothing¶

Smoothing a time series is useful in discovering certain traits in a time series, such as long-term trend an seasonal components. As we seen before, we can smooth a time series in a basic version using the moving average smoother.

$$ M_t = \sum_{j=-k}^k a_j X_{t-j} $$

where $a_j = a_{-j} \geq 0$ and $\sum_{j=-k}^k a_j = 1$ is a symetric moving average of the data.

Kernel Smoothing¶

Kernel smoothing is a moving average smoother that uses a weight function, or kernel, to average the observations, it is

$$ M_t = \sum_{i=1}^{T} w_i(t) X_i $$

where

$$ w_i(t) = \frac{K\left(\frac{t-i}{b}\right)}{ \sum_{j=1}^{T} K\left(\frac{t-j}{b}\right)} $$

are the weights and $K(\cdot)$ is a kernel function.

Stationarity¶

Stationarity is an important concept in time series analysis.

  • Stationarity means that the statistical properties of a time series (or rather the process generating it) do not change over time.

  • Stationarity is important because many useful analytical tools and statistical tests and models rely on it.

As such, the ability to determine if a time series is stationary is important. Rather than deciding between two strict options, this usually means being able to ascertain, with high probability, that a series is generated by a stationary process.

Looking at the Data¶

The most basic methods for stationarity detection rely on plotting the data, or functions of it, and determining visually whether they present some known property of stationary (or non-stationary) data.

Removing Trends¶

As mentioned before, the stationarity property is useful in the time series analysis, then we need some tools that could help us to achive this in a non-stationary time series.

Trend Stationary¶

Perhaps the easiest form of nonstationarity to work with is the trend stationary model wherein the process has stationary behavior around a trend. We may write this type of model as

$$ X_t = \mu_t + Y_t $$

where $X_t$ are the observations, $\mu_t$ denotes the trend, and $Y_t$ is a stationary process. Quite often, strong trend will obscure the behavior of the stationary process, $Y_t$, hence, there is some advantage to removing the trend as a rst step in an exploratory analysis of such time series.

We might model trend as a stochastic component using the random walk with drift model

$$ \mu_t = \delta + \mu_{t-1} + W_t $$

If the appropriate model is $X_t = \mu_t + Y_t$, then dierencing the data, $X_t$, yields a stationary process;

$$ X_t - X_{t-1} = (\mu_t + Y_t) - (\mu_{t-1} + Y_{t-1}) \\ = (\delta + \mu_{t-1} + W_t + Y_t) - (\mu_{t-1} + Y_{t-1}) \\ = \delta + W_t + Y_t - Y_{t-1} $$

where $Y_t - Y_{t-1}$ is a stationary process.

Seasonal Stationary¶

Another kind of nonstationarity is a seasonal behavior. We may write this type of model as before

$$ X_t = \mu_t + Y_t $$

but in this case, we might model the trend as a stochastic component using the signal in noise model

$$ \mu_t = A cos\left(2 \pi \omega t + \phi \right) = A cos\left(2 \pi \omega \left[t - \frac{1}{\omega} \right] + \phi \right) = \mu_{t-\frac{1}{\omega}} $$

Then, the differences must be at each $t-\frac{1}{\omega}$ to get a stationary process, this is

$$ X_t - X_{t-\frac{1}{\omega}} = \left( \mu_t + Y_t \right) - \left( \mu_{t-\frac{1}{\omega}} + Y_{t-\frac{1}{\omega}}\right) = Y_t - Y_{t-\frac{1}{\omega}} $$

Where $Y_t - Y_{t-\frac{1}{\omega}}$ is a stationary process.

Differencing¶

Because dierencing plays a central role in time series analysis, it receives its own notation. Before introduce formally the differences, we define the backshift operator (or lag operator) as

$$ B X_t = X_{t-1} $$

and extend it to powers $B^2 X_t = B(B X_t) = B X_{t-1} = X_{t-2}$, and so on. Thus

$$ B^k X_t = X_{t-k} $$

The idea of an inverse operator can also be given if we require $B^{-1} B$=1, so that

$$ X_t = B^{-1}B X_t = B^{-1} X_{t1} $$

That is, $B^{-1}$ is the forward-shift operator.

Then, we can express a difference of order $d$ in terms of the backshift operator as

$$ \nabla^d=(1-B)^d $$

where we may expand the operator $(1 B)^d$ algebraically to evaluate for higher integer values of $d$.
